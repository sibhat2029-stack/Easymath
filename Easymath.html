Chapter One – Number System

1.1 Introduction of Numbers

Numbers are fundamental mathematical objects used for counting, measuring, and labeling. Natural numbers are denoted by N. They are used for counting objects and ordering items in a series.

Set Comparison:

All natural numbers are whole numbers (proper subset), but 0 is a whole number and not a natural number.

Properties of Natural Numbers

Closure under addition and multiplication:
The sum or product of any two natural numbers is always a natural number.
Examples:
2 + 3 = 5
2 × 3 = 6

Not closed under subtraction:
2 − 5 = −3 (not a natural number)

Not closed under division:
5 ÷ 2 = 2.5
5 ÷ 4 = 1.25

Examples:
Counting: You have 3 potatoes.
Ordering: It is the 5th clever student.
Labeling: The jersey number is 10.

1.3 Whole Numbers

Definition: Whole numbers are natural numbers plus zero.
Examples: 0, 1, 2, 3, …

They do not include fractions, decimals, or negative numbers.

Relationship to Other Sets:
Natural Numbers: N = {1,2,3,...}
Integers: Z = {...,−2,−1,0,1,2,...}
Smallest Whole Number: 0

Operations on Whole Numbers

Addition:
Closed under addition. Example: 5 + 3 = 8
Commutative: 4 + 7 = 7 + 4
Associative: (2 + 3) + 5 = 2 + (3 + 5)
Identity: a + 0 = a

Subtraction:
Not closed. Example: 3 − 5 = −2

Multiplication:
Closed under multiplication.
Commutative, associative, identity (1), zero property.

Division:
Not closed.
7 ÷ 2 = 3.5 (not whole)
Division by zero is undefined.

1.4 Rational Numbers

Definition: A rational number can be written as p/q where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0.
Symbol: Q

Examples:
5 = 5/1
−3 = (−3)/1
0 = 0/1

Review Questions

5. What type of number is √7? Why?
